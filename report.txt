3. REFLECTION / DESIGN
------------------------------------------------------------------------

(A) INTERACTIVE PROCESS / SYSTEM IMAGE DESIGN

(a) Follow-instructions process — feedback on progress
    The purchase flow is Cart -> Checkout -> Confirmation. To always show
    the user where they are, what is done, and what remains, the design
    uses TWO complementary indicators:

      1. A macro progress stepper (component: CheckoutSteps) pinned to
         the top of the Cart, Checkout, and Confirmation pages. Completed
         steps show a green check mark, the current step is highlighted
         with a ring, and upcoming steps are muted and numbered. Screen
         readers receive "completed / current step / not yet started"
         via visually-hidden text and aria-current="step".

      2. Inside the single-page Checkout, the form is split into clearly
         numbered sub-sections — (1) Contact, (2) Shipping address,
         (3) Payment — so the micro-steps within "Checkout" are also
         explicit. A persistent Order Summary (subtotal, shipping, tax,
         total) stays visible throughout so the user never loses context,
         and the final Confirmation page restates the order number,
         shipping address, and estimated delivery window.

(b) Explore process — faceted-search filters
    The Shop page implements a functional faceted search. The facets were
    chosen from a plant buyer's semantic network — the questions a person
    actually asks when choosing a tree:

      - Category (where/what it is): Indoor, Outdoor, Fruit, Bonsai,
        Tree Care Supplies.
      - Care level (can I keep it alive?): Easy, Moderate, Advanced.
      - Availability: "In stock only".
      - Free-text search across product name, description, and category.
      - Sort: Featured, Price (low->high, high->low), Top rated, Name A-Z.

    Filters combine (AND logic), update live, and are stored in the URL
    query string, so a filtered view is shareable and bookmarkable and
    survives a page refresh. A live result count ("N products") gives
    immediate feedback, and an empty state suggests broadening the search.

(c) Communicate process — a non-intrusive, engaging survey
    The survey (page: /survey) is the communicate process. It is designed
    to be non-intrusive and engaging:

      - Opt-in, never a pop-up. It is reached by a warm call-to-action on
        the Order Confirmation page ("How did we do?") and a "Share
        feedback" link in the footer — the user is invited, not blocked.
      - Short (under a minute) and low-effort: an emoji rating scale, two
        quick multiple-choice questions (one about how easy it was to
        find items, tying back to the faceted search), and one OPTIONAL
        open comment. Only the overall rating is required.
      - Reassuring: labelled "Anonymous & secure — no account needed."
      - Rewarding: submitting shows a cheerful thank-you screen
        ("You just made our day!") that routes the user back to shopping.


(B) VERBAL COMMUNICATION DESIGN

Writer/Reader model
    The default model is "TreeHaven as a warm, knowledgeable nursery
    (we) speaking to a plant lover (you)". The brand voice is calm,
    encouraging, and expert-but-approachable. Consistency is maintained
    by always addressing the reader in the second person ("your cart",
    "help your trees flourish") and by keeping a single friendly-expert
    persona across every page and the shared navbar/footer.

    Local "conversation effects": the tone shifts to a more personal,
    first-person and exclamatory register at emotional high points — the
    order Confirmation greets the customer by first name ("Thank you,
    [Name]!") and the survey speaks intimately and celebrates the user
    ("We'd love to hear from you!", "You just made our day!"). These
    localised shifts create warmth without breaking overall consistency.

Three communicative purposes (with examples)

    1. Incite to action (promotional, imperative/exclamatory)
       - Hero: "Bring the calm of nature into every space" +
         button "Shop the collection".
       - CTA banner: "New to plant parenthood?" -> "Shop easy-care trees".
       - Cart nudge: "You're $X away from free shipping!"
       - Urgency/promo badges: "Bestseller", "Only 5 left", "New".

    2. Inform (neutral, declarative)
       - Product care specs: "Light: Bright, indirect light",
         "Water: When top 2 inches of soil are dry",
         "Care level: Moderate", "Size: 3-4 ft tall on arrival".
       - Order summary line items and the FAQ answers (shipping times,
         thresholds, guarantee terms) use plain, factual sentences.

    3. Engage in a connection (positive, interrogative/exclamatory,
       inclusive "we/you")
       - Survey: "How was your shopping experience?",
         "Would you recommend TreeHaven to a friend?"
       - Newsletter: "Stay rooted" / "Growing guides and new arrivals,
         once a month. No spam."
       - Contact: "We'd love to hear from you."

4. HIGH-FIDELITY PROTOTYPE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

(a) Visual design choices
    - Colour theme: A cohesive natural palette expressed as CSS design
      tokens — forest greens (primary actions, headings), sage greens
      (surfaces, highlights), earthy bark browns, and cream/beige
      neutrals (page background). A single warm amber accent is reserved
      for promotional CTAs and badges so "buy" moments stand out.
    - Typography: A serif display face (Iowan/Palatino stack) for
      headings paired with a clean system sans-serif for body text. The
      contrast between the two attracts attention to headings and builds
      a clear type hierarchy (eyebrow label -> H1/H2 -> lede -> body).
    - Layout & negative space: A constrained max-width container with
      generous section padding and airy card grids keeps pages calm and
      scannable and lets the botanical illustrations breathe.
    - Contrast, scale, balance, hierarchy: Dark forest text on cream
      gives strong contrast; fluid (clamp) heading sizes create scale;
      two-column layouts (hero, product detail, checkout) are balanced by
      a sticky order/summary panel; prices and primary buttons sit high
      in the visual hierarchy.
    - Gestalt principles: Similarity (all products use an identical card
      pattern), proximity (care specs grouped in one tinted panel),
      continuity (aligned responsive grids), closure (circular icon
      badges), and common region (cards and panels group related content).
    - Visual/verbal synergy: The green palette and hand-built botanical
      SVG product illustrations reinforce the "calm, natural, eco" voice,
      so what the user sees and what they read tell the same story.
    - Self-contained SVG artwork means no broken images and no external
      image requests — the prototype works fully offline.

    Tech: Built with React (Vite), React Router for multi-page
    navigation, React Context + useReducer for cart state (persisted to
    localStorage so the cart survives refreshes), and plain CSS driven by
    design tokens. The project passes lint with 0 warnings/0 errors and
    builds cleanly.

6. HEURISTIC EVALUATION (Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
For each heuristic, an element of TreeHaven that is particularly in line
with it. (Attach a small screenshot of each referenced element.)

1. Visibility of system status
   The Cart -> Checkout -> Confirmation progress stepper, the cart-count
   badge in the navbar, the "Added to cart" confirmation on buttons, the
   live "N products" count in Shop, and the free-shipping progress bar
   all keep the user continuously informed of the current state.
   [Screenshot: checkout progress stepper]

2. Match between the system and the real world
   Language and icons match the user's world: a leaf logo, a shopping-cart
   icon, and plant vocabulary such as "Care level", "Light: bright,
   indirect", "30-day thrive guarantee", plus a Home > Shop > Category >
   Product breadcrumb that mirrors how people navigate a store.
   [Screenshot: product care panel]

3. User control and freedom
   Users can remove a line item, clear the whole cart, adjust quantities,
   use "Back to cart" on checkout, "Clear all filters" in Shop, and the
   404 page offers clear exits home. Nothing is a dead end.
   [Screenshot: cart with remove / clear controls]

4. Consistency and standards
   A shared design-token system and reusable components (buttons, cards,
   the Order Summary reused on Cart/Checkout/Confirmation, one navbar and
   footer sitewide) keep everything consistent; the cart icon sits top-
   right where users expect it.
   [Screenshot: navbar + a reused card/button]

5. Error prevention
   Quantities are clamped to available stock (you cannot exceed it), the
   Add button is disabled for out-of-stock items, the card-number and
   MM/YY expiry fields auto-format to prevent malformed input, and the
   Checkout page redirects to the Cart if there are no items to purchase.
   [Screenshot: quantity stepper at stock limit / disabled Add]

6. Recognition rather than recall
   Persistent navigation and breadcrumbs, an always-visible order summary
   during checkout, a cart that persists across refreshes, "You may also
   like" related products, and category tiles all let users recognise
   options instead of remembering them.
   [Screenshot: checkout with persistent order summary]

7. Flexibility and efficiency of use
   The faceted search stores state in the URL (shareable/bookmarkable),
   there is a global search box in the navbar, sort options for power
   users, one-click "Add" straight from a product card, and autocomplete
   attributes on checkout fields to speed up form entry.
   [Screenshot: Shop filters + URL query]

8. Aesthetic and minimalist design
   A calm palette and generous negative space keep pages focused;
   progressive disclosure hides complexity until needed — FAQ answers are
   collapsible, "Billing same as shipping" hides the billing fields, and
   most survey questions are optional.
   [Screenshot: FAQ accordion or checkout billing toggle]

9. Help users recognise, diagnose, and recover from errors
   Forms use inline, human-readable validation messages (e.g. "Enter a
   valid 5-digit ZIP code", "That card number looks invalid"), mark
   fields with aria-invalid, and move keyboard focus to the first error
   so problems are easy to find and fix.
   [Screenshot: checkout field showing an inline error]

10. Help and documentation
    A dedicated FAQ page, per-product care instructions, a Contact page,
    inline field hints, and a helpful demo-payment note on Checkout
    ("Use test card 4242 4242 4242 4242...") give users the guidance they
    need without leaving the flow.
    [Screenshot: FAQ page or checkout demo-payment hint]
